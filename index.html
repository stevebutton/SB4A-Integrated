The Sustainable Business for Africa (SB4A) platform is an initiative to help EU staff in headquarters and in African delegations engage in a structured dialogue with the local and European private sector.
This interactive application will provide you with concrete examples and advice on public-private dialogue. Explore to find practical tips on how to implement public-private dialogue, understand key concepts and policy background.

01 key concepts

There are three key concepts that drive the implementation and success of the SB4A.
They are Private Sector Engagement (PSE), Structured Dialogue and Public-Private Dialogue.

Understanding each is a necessary step to implement SB4A initiatives.

Private Sector Engagement

Private Sector Engagement in South Africa

Massimo de Luca
Economic Partnership Agreements -ACP Countries DG Trade

watch the video

Private Sector Engagement refers to activities of donors that enable and encourage private sector actors to contribute to development objectives.

PSE can lead to an improved investment climate and potentially contribute to achieving a more inclusive growth.

Indeed, by enabling and setting incentives for investments in low-income communities and sectors that specifically benefit the poor, the EU can provide incentives for the private sector to operate towards poor communities.

Structured Dialogue

Structured Dialogue in Uganda

Massimo Diomedi Camassei, Trade & private sector development adviser EUD Uganda

watch the video

Structured Dialogue describes the process of engaging with stakeholders in a planned, regular, timely, predictable and transparent manner.

It is used in contrast to “ad-hoc consultations” as stakeholders are invited to provide their input in a well-planned set-up, and they receive feedback of the outcomes of the consultations as well as information about the overall process of dialogue.

It is also different from “one-off consultations”, where the EU seeks a specific input from the private sector.

Public-Private Dialogue

Public-Private Dialogue  - DEVCO Academy)

François Kacen, TPSD Facility Expert

watch the PRESENTATION

Public-Private Dialogue (PPD) is a type of structured dialogue. It is a systematic, planned, participatory, and inclusive approach to policymaking at its best.

It is directed at reforming the business environment and the investment climate, especially where other policy institutions are underperforming.

Dialogue improves the flow of information relating to economic policy and builds legitimacy into the policy process.

It also seeks to overcome impediments to transparency and accommodate greater inclusion of stakeholders in decision-making.

The benefits of Public-Private Dialogues are not immediate but span in the long run.

Indeed, frequent consultations with the private sector help governments to prioritise reforms, and secure confidence from investors in a long-term perspective, and enable businesses to develop.

It should also inform on the impact of possible measures on the wide spectrum of the private sector, paying a particular attention to the most vulnerable.

02 facilitating ppd

Facilitating dialogue between public and private stakeholders is not an easy task. But with some method and organisation, it can be powerful and rewarding. To ensure efficient public-private dialogue facilitation, EU Delegations must remember five success factors:
1. Working with Champions
2. Inclusiveness
3. Locally owned
4. Sustainability
5. Capacity

Facilitating PPD: Working with Champions

Public-Private Dialogue in Madagascar

Céline Maertens,
Team Leader, Southern Africa & Indian Ocean Geo Desk, DEVCO

watch the video

The first success factor is to identify and work with Champions from the public and private sectors, to let them take ownership of the dialogue process.

Putting Champions at the forefront of PPD will allow the whole process to gain credibility and lead to stronger recognition.Public sector champions are partners with strong leadership and buy-in. Private sector champions are business representative that are independent and have the ability to represent a group or community.

The dialogue will be as inclusive as possible, reaching out and taking in consideration independent structures, supporting unorganised interests to participate and accomodating and representing the diversity of private sector interests.

All kinds of businesses should be reached out to and included in the discussions, be they formal and informal, large and small, local or international.

Facilitating PPD: Locally Owned

Public-private dialogue processes need to be locally owned.
The role of a donor is to support and facilitate local structures and actors, not to replace them or create “external” structures.

Facilitating PPD: Sustainability

To be sustainable, a public-private dialogue process must meet operational and financial sustainability and have a sustained mandate.
In the case of a donor supporting PPD and once it meets these 3 sustainability pillars, the donor can exit the process successfully. It must be however acknowledged that processes are long and that organisations at grass root level need usually support over several years.

Facilitating PPD: Capacity

Public-Private Dialogue, The small business perspective

Chloé Allio, DG DEVCO,
Olivier Coupleux DG TRADE, Filip Tack, Entrepreneur

PPD stakeholder, be they public or private, need strong capacities to provide coherent policy recommendations, negotiate constructively, analyse evidence and nurture the on-going dialogue.

On the private sector side, organisations must be in capacity to propose evidence-based reforms and coordinate businesses’ interests, while public sector representatives must have the capacity and resources to analyse, formulate coherent reforms, implement and monitor the reforms and communicate with stakeholders.

03 facilitating ppd in fragile contexts

Public-Private Dialogue (PPD) is particularly useful in fragile and conflict affected states (FCAS) to mitigate entrenched interests, rebuild trust and spur inclusive and sustainable growth. Yet, PPD Facilitation is even more complex and challenging, and calls for specific actions.

Facilitating PPD in fragile contexts

Facilitating PPD in Fragile countries and contexts

François Kacen,
TPSD Facility Expert

READ THE DOCUMENT

Practitioners have identified common constraints deteriorating the business environment in FCAS and discouraging much-needed investment:

  • Higher informality of the private sector originating from poor regulation capacity of the governments and weak access to finance;
  • Access to land problems;
  • Political instability, unpredictability and security issues;
  • Weak private sector technical and managerial capacity;
  • dysfunctional judiciary system;
  • Widespread mistrust and impaired value chains.

04 facilitating ppd in covid-19 times

The coronavirus pandemic that started late 2019 / early 2020 and the restrictive measures taken all over the world to reduce the spread of the virus have had dramatic impact on our global economies, and in particular on those of developing countries.

Facilitating PPD in Covid-19 Times

EU Thematic Brief PSE in covid times

EU Thematic Brief PSE in covid times, DEVCO

read the document

The private sector can bring valuable contributions to address the sanitary and socio-economic impact of the outbreak, and governments and development partners should more than ever actively look for ways to diminish the impact of the crisis on businesses and job losses, and pave the way to a quicker and sustainable economy recovery.

Supporting PPD for Business Environment Reform is considered as a useful tool allowing Governments and development partners to assess the impact of the COVID19 epidemic on the economy, but also as a necessity to adopt the much-needed emergency measures to support businesses operations continuity.

PPD is a key element to cope with the current situation. Special attention should be given to identify and address the informal sector’s constraints.

Next: Supporting Inclusivity

06 supporting inclusivity

Private sector is diverse, and public-private consultations should reflect this granularity. Marginalised groups such as women entrepreneurs, youth entrepreneurs and the informal sector, form major parts of economic activities but are frequently unorganised and under-represented in PPDs. Some businesses have a greater capacity to represent themselves within these processes than others: large, urban, formal sector, male-managed firms have more important financial and organisational capacity to participate than smaller, rural, informal, and women-owned firms.

Including Women

Including Women Placeholder Document

Credit & Title go here

READ THE DOCUMENT

Empowering women entrepreneurs to identify the constrains they face in their business life and supporting them to constructively participate to PPD is key to correct gender inequalities affecting the private sector.

Integrating a gender perspective into PPDs can occur at two levels:

a) At policy and action level, a gender lens can be applied to a PPD’s outputs and outcomes, whether these are about legal or regulatory reform or about undertaking a specific set of actions;

b) At PPD implementation level, applying a gender lens aims to ensure that women are equally represented in the dialogue process.

Including Informal Sector

Including the Informal Sector -Placeholder

Credit & Title go here

READ THE DOCUMENT

In developing countries, where the informal sector is dominant, it is crucial that small, micro and informal entrepreneurs participate to public-private consultations, and their capacities should be built to allow them to express their interests in a coherent and analytical manner.

Informal worker associations do exist in some countries and represent sectors such as petty traders and small street vendors. Cooperatives or social groups are also important actors to be considered and can represent an important number of informal enterprises and workers.

According to the OECD, two types of interventions should be implemented to support MSMEs and informal sector to participate to BER:

a) supporting the establishment and development of intermediary organisations and cooperatives representing micro and informal enterprises and stimulating linkages and cooperation between them;

b) encouraging existing private sector organisations to become more representative and strengthening their understanding of micro and informal enterprises’ constraints67.

Including Grassroot Organisations

Including Grassroot Organisations -Placeholder

Grassroot organisations are often neglected in talks related to the business environment reforms.

Letting them participate to PPD is challenging as these have weak organisational structure, are likely to be unable to institutionalise knowledge, and their financial sustainability is low since their members are informal enterprises.

Next: Monitoring & Evaluation

07 monitoring & evaluation

According to the PPD Charter, Monitoring & Evaluation (M&E)  is an effective tool to manage the public-private dialogue process and to demonstrate its purpose and performance. The advantages of monitoring and evaluation are numerous: M&E techniques improve overall planning of PPDs, facilitate advocacy, and increase incentives to promote more effective implementation.

Monitoring & Evaluation

Monitoring and Evaluation methods for Public-Private Dialogue

François Kacen,
TPSD Facility Expert

watch the PRESENTATION

M&E mechanisms should remain simple and flexible in order to serve as a tool to promote transparency and accountability. A participatory approach is considered as a best practice to implement M&E of PPDs, and all stakeholders should be involved. M&E should not only cover enactment of reforms, but also scrutinize their implementation after adoption.

Finally, targeted inputs, outputs, outcomes and impacts should be defined with specific indicators and reviewed periodically by PPD stakeholders.

The objective is to identify a methodology based on international practice and adapt it to the needs of SB4A.
Next: Policy Background

08 policy background

The approaches and policies involved with the SB4A have evolved through the experiences among and dialogue between key actors from the public and private sectors. Lets examine the background of the key policies and developments that have shaped the SB4A agenda.

2014 Communication on Private Sector & SB4A

SB4A: an essential building block of the External Investment Plan

Antti Karhunen,
Head of Unit, DEVCO C4

watch the video

In 2014, the EC communication

‘A Stronger Role of the Private Sector in Achieving Inclusive and Sustainable Growth in Developing Countries’

recognised the private sector as a key partner to create jobs, contribute to poverty alleviation, support innovation and technology transfer, and bring financial resources into partner countries.

The EC will look for new ways of harnessing the potential of the private sector as a financing partner, implementing agent, advisor or intermediary to achieve more effective and efficient delivery of EU support, not only in the field of local private sector development, but also in other areas of EU development cooperation such as sustainable energy, sustainable agriculture and agribusiness, digital and physical infrastructure, and the green and social sectors.

In that same Communication, the EC foresees that EU Delegations will play an active role in enabling inclusive public-private policy dialogue by

“supporting the functioning of existing or new dialogue mechanisms such as national employment, labour or export councils, and by targeting capacity building to private sector representatives, including chambers of commerce, social partners, and organizations representing micro, small and medium-sized enterprises, female entrepreneurs, and firms and workers in the informal sector, to improve their contribution to such dialogue mechanisms”.

2017: The External Investment Plan and SB4A

The EIP in Practice,
E-learning module

watch the video

In 2017, the European Union adopted the European Investment Plan (EIP) to remove constraints to private investment in partner country markets and to support priority reforms.

The EIP provides a comprehensive and integrated approach, linking investment mobilization (pillar 1) with technical assistance (pillar 2) and investment climate improvement (pillar 3) ..
Three building blocks compose Pilar 3 and are closely interlinked:
 1) investment climate analysis, enhancing market intelligence with evidence-based data and country analysis,  and identification of investment opportunities in key sectors and value chains – Jobs and Growth Compacts

‍

2) engaging in structured public-private dialogue, with private sector engagement and structured dialogue for perspective on investment climate and business constraints – SB4A;

‍

3) interventions to support priority reforms, capacity building of public and private sector, value addition and entrepreneurship, to address investors’ perceived risks.

SB4A is a key element of the External investment Plan.

The facilitation of structured public-private dialogue is one of the building blocks of the EIP’s Investment clime pilar (Pilar 3).

10 state of play

Explore this section and its country profiles to learn about the structured dialogue mechanisms currently in place in Africa, their efficiency and inclusivity, and the involvement of each EU Delegation to support public-private dialogue.

Key Takeaways

The 2019 State of Play shows a positive trend towards SB4A uptake by EU Delegations, although this uptake still needed to be encouraged and actively supported. Many disparities exist throughout Africa in terms of PPD effectiveness and inclusiveness, and some EUDs had not yet started to facilitate public-private dialogue mechanisms. Main takeaways of the 2019 survey:
PPD is widespread in Africa: 77% of surveyed countries have an established PPD, Yet the mechanisms in place show a rather low effectiveness (3.9/10) in terms of investment climate improvement.

Fragile states have few or poorly effective PPD mechanisms

‍

82% of EUDs already have regular contacts with the private sector and 31%  are already involved in facilitating PPD.
Large companies and the financial sector predominate PPDs, while micro and informal enterprises tend to be underrepresented.
Governments tend to dominate the participation in public-private consultation mechanisms, with the risk that they capture them. Women and youth are underrepresented in PPDs.
